Trisecting Arbitrary Angles

lol, I messed with some obtuse angles, and the method falls apart spectacularly.
I couldn't see it when I was still fiddling with acute angles.
as I expected, the idea was deeply flawed :)

I don't use a rule and compass often, and it turns out what looked like a ridged tightener on the top of the compass I bought was fake, much to my annoyance. Please forgive minor drafting errors. I am trying to demonstrate the technique, rather than my ability at it.

I was watching a video[1] earlier today, that mentioned that not only was it unknown how to trisect an arbitrary angle with only a rule and compass, but that it was proven impossible in the mid-1800s.

I thought this odd, as the same video demonstrated a technique that, so far as I can tell, should be able to trisect an arbitrary angle with ease.

This requires no measurements, and only the use of a straight edge and compass.

Let us create an arbitrary angle to trisect.
First an initial point.

And then two more points of varying distances from the original.
Please forgive the blurriness of a few of these images.

Okay, now that we have our two lines, we set our compass to an arbitrary angle, and walk each of the lines from the initial point, putting three marks on each.

Here is a link to the image for just that step, but it's the blurriest of them all.
Rather just see the marks in the next step.

Next we draw a line between the third points of the two lines.

Now we need to create some parallel lines. To do this we set another arbitrary angle on our compass, marking the original point, and the first and second points on the top line.

Now we use our compass to take the distance of between the points where the circle segment by the original angle crosses. We use this setting to mark the segments we drew after the first and second points to create the same angle at each of them.

Now we draw parallel lines from the first and second point using these.

Now we can draw lines from the origin through the points where the parallel lines cross our line between the equidistance third points on the lines.

The angle should now be trisected.

  • We create a line three times an arbitrary width on each original side.
  • This creates an isosceles triangle, with known thirds on a side.
  • We then transfer these thirds to the unknown side via the parallel lines of the well known line trisection technique. [2]
  • Since we now know where points a third into that line are, we can draw lines from the original point through them.
  • This divides the original angle into three equal angles.

Now let's build three congruent circles across the line

We'll start by finding the midpoints between the two four points on the end line.
First we use arcs to find points on the lines.

And then we can draw in the lines to find the midpoints on the end-line.

Now I draw a circle from the middle point, to show the other two points are equidistant from it.

Now I set the compass to the radius from one side to the first dot, and use it to draw three congruent circles that kiss at their edges.

They are admittedly imperfect, but this should be due moreso to the wobble of my hand than the method of their construction.

I look forward to finding out why this won't work :)

It looks to me like it should, though. It appears it could be extended to arbitrarily dividing the original angle since you can use an arbitrary number of points and therefore parallel lines in the construction.
